The Details

First, a little back story. The Connexus Business Incubator teamed up with Hack Regina and InnovationSask to bring the first annual 24 hour Start-Up Competition. The idea is that whole bunch of up and coming programmers and designers get together to pitch their ideas for a start-up company. You get 60 seconds (yes, only sixty seconds!) to wow everyone with your pitch. Afterwards, the rag-tag group of innovators vote on which ideas are strong enough to move on. If your pitch moves on, you get to recruit team members and once your team is formed, you have 24 hours to plan, design and build a minimum viable product that, if you make it far enough, you get to pitch to actual real-world investors (think Dragons Den style).

I thought this would be the perfect platform to share an idea that would eventually morph into 'Aliss'. In short, I wanted to harness the power of machine learning to receive, analyze and translate real-time brain activity into a signal that a computer program can understand and use. Whether it is used to control a character in a Virtual Reality video game, or a way for someone afflicted by a debilitating neurological disorder to communicate and connect with their outside world in a way that they no longer can.

Turns out, others liked it as much as I did and it was picked up to move on.

My Roles

Recruitment

The first task I had was to recruit my team. I needed some skilled coders willing to take on such an ambitious goal and some creative minds for beautification. After some impromptu 'interviews', I was able to select 4 very skilled people: 3 programmers with backgrounds in data analysis and machine learning(ML), and a designer with a business background that would work great for the market research that we would need.

Sharpening our Vision

With time being a limited resource, we needed to quickly hash out what our final product was going to be. What was attainable within the time-frame while still achieving our lofty goals. Based on team member strengths, we decided on the technologies we would use, and created 3 base goals (a robust ML backend, an easy to use Interface, and a target market) to meet that we would blend together to make our final product.

Delegating Assignments

With our goals set out, it was time for the grind. I Matched up team members to appropriate tasks, once again, based on what I viewed their areas of expertise to be. I assigned 2 of our programmers with researching and developing the backend, while another programmer and I would get to work on the product branding and design of the frontend. I had our business designer start market research to find out what was out there in the industry and where we would fit in. Game on.

Design and Branding

I am a big fan of science fiction movies (and you can't deny that this project smells a little science fiction-y!). I also love giving nods to some of those movies in my projects. Aliss pays homage to one of my favourite movie franchises and characters: Project Alice from Resident Evil. I did some wordsmithing to make it work as Aliss stands for 'Advanced Learning Integration Software Solutions'. Once the name was in place, the logo and main branding just popped out and all that was left was choosing an attractive colour pallette and working it in all throughout the user interface.

Constant Re-evaluation

Steadily checking in on the other team members to gauge our progress so far, and where we were headed. Being able to identify problem areas and pivot ideas, reassigning goals on the fly, turned out to be a crucial cornerstone to our success.

Presentation

We had made it through to the final 5 start-ups, which meant that we would need to present a Minimum Viable Product to the judges. In 3 minutes. Better than 60 seconds, but come on! We were able to pack in as much information into a fairly flashy presentation. However, it wasn't enough for us to bring home the prize, but I could not be happier with how far we had made it, and the progress that we achieved in such a short time.
